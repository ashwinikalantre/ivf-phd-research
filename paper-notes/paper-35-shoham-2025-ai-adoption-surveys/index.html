    Paper 35: Global AI Adoption Trends Among Fertility Specialists¶

    Citation¶

    Shoham, G., Alexandroni, H., Weissman, A., & Mizrachi, Y. (2025). Global trends in the use of artificial intelligence (AI) in reproductive medicine: insights from surveys of international fertility specialists. Journal of IVF-Worldwide, 3(3), 33-44.

    DOI: https://doi.org/10.46989/001c.140673

    Source checked: Journal of IVF-Worldwide full text

    Evidence note:

    This is a full-text survey article. Extraction confidence is high for survey design, respondent counts, adoption percentages, perceived benefits, barriers and risks.

    Related study terms:

    • IVF
    • ART
    • Embryo Selection
    • Clinical Decision Support System
    • Trustworthy AI
    • Regulatory Oversight
    • Clinical Utility

    Why This Paper Matters¶

    This paper is different from model-development papers. It does not ask whether one algorithm performs well. It asks whether fertility professionals are actually using AI, where they are using it, and what prevents adoption.

    For our PhD, this paper is very useful because it supports the real-world deployment argument:

    • AI use in IVF is increasing
    • embryo selection is still the dominant use case
    • cost and training are major barriers
    • clinicians worry about over-reliance, errors and privacy
    • many clinics still lack enough evidence to judge AI impact

    This helps us explain why a PhD topic should include trust, explainability, clinical workflow and clinician usability, not only prediction accuracy.

    Research Objective¶

    The study aimed to compare two global surveys of IVF specialists and embryologists conducted in 2022 and 2025 to assess AI adoption, application areas, perceptions, barriers and future investment interest in reproductive medicine.

    Confidence: High

    Study Design and Dataset¶

    Item Extracted Information
    Article type Original survey research
    Journal Journal of IVF-Worldwide
    Published 7 July 2025
    Survey platform IVF-Worldwide.com
    Survey type Global web-based questionnaires
    Survey waves July-August 2022 and February-March 2025
    Invitations 3,890 registered IVF units
    2022 complete responses 383
    2025 analyzed responses 171
    Respondents Physicians, fertility specialists, embryologists/scientists, industry professionals and others
    Geographic coverage Six continents
    Analysis Descriptive statistics, chi-square tests, Fisher's exact tests, subgroup descriptive analysis
    Software SPSS version 27
    Patient-level data None

    Confidence: High

    Respondent Profile¶

    Group 2022 2025
    Physicians/fertility specialists 64.8% (n=248) 56.73% (n=97)
    Embryologists/scientists 30.0% (n=115) 35.09% (n=60)
    Industry professionals Not listed 3.51% (n=6)
    Other professionals 5.2% (n=20) 4.68% (n=8)

    Regional pattern:

    • Europe had the highest representation in 2022.
    • Asia had the highest representation in 2025.
    • Respondents came from Europe, Asia, South America, USA/Canada, Africa and Australia/New Zealand, although Australia/New Zealand had no respondents in 2025.

    Confidence: High

    AI Adoption and Usage¶

    Adoption / Use Item 2022 2025
    AI use 24.8% (n=95) 53.22% regular or occasional use (n=91)
    Regular AI use Not separated 21.64% (n=37)
    Occasional AI use Not separated 31.58% (n=54)
    Rarely use AI Not separated 17.54% (n=30)
    Never use AI 75.2% (n=288) 29.24% (n=50)
    Usage increase p-value Not applicable p < 0.0001

    Primary application:

    • In 2022, embryo selection was reported by 86.3% of AI users.
    • In 2025, embryo selection remained the most common application, reported by 32.75% of all respondents.

    Important caution:

    The 2022 and 2025 embryo-selection percentages use different denominators, so they should not be interpreted as a simple decline.

    Confidence: High

    Familiarity, Benefits and Investment Interest¶

    Item Finding
    2025 at least moderate AI familiarity 60.82% (n=104)
    2025 very familiar 24.56% (n=42)
    2025 somewhat familiar 36.26% (n=62)
    2022 top perceived benefit Embryo selection, 91.6% (n=351)
    2025 top perceived benefit Embryo selection, 38.6% (n=66)
    2025 workflow benefit 22.22% (n=38)
    2025 education benefit 15.79% (n=27)
    2025 likely to invest in AI within 1-5 years 83.62% (n=143)

    Confidence: High

    Barriers and Risks¶

    Important reported barriers in 2025:

    Barrier / Risk 2025 Finding
    Cost 38.01% (n=65)
    Lack of training 33.92% (n=58)
    Ethical/legal concerns 14.04% (n=24)
    Over-reliance on technology 59.06% (n=101)
    Misdiagnosis/errors 22.81% (n=39)
    Privacy 11.11% (n=19)

    The paper reports that cost and training became dominant adoption concerns in 2025, while ethical and legal risks also became more visible.

    Confidence: High

    Outcome and Clinical Utility Perceptions¶

    In 2025:

    • 29.83% reported improved patient outcomes from AI use.
    • 32.75% reported no AI use, so they could not assess outcomes.
    • 20.47% said they lacked data to assess impact.
    • 54.38% rated AI embryo-selection accuracy as moderately or very accurate.
    • 32.16% were unsure about embryo-selection accuracy.

    This is very useful for our PhD because it shows the clinical-utility evidence gap from the user's side: many specialists are interested, but many still lack enough evidence or experience to judge real benefit.

    Confidence: High

    Method / Algorithm¶

    This paper does not develop a machine learning model.

    Item Extracted Information
    Method type Survey analysis
    Statistical methods Frequencies, percentages, chi-square tests, Fisher's exact tests
    Machine learning model Not applicable
    Prediction metrics Not applicable
    Patient dataset Not applicable

    Confidence: High

    Limitations Stated By Authors / Study Design¶

    Limitation Meaning
    Unknown participant overlap The same people may or may not have answered both surveys.
    Selection bias possible People interested in AI may have been more likely to respond.
    Different survey structures Some 2022 and 2025 comparisons require caution.
    Web-based voluntary survey Results may not represent all IVF clinics globally.
    No patient outcome validation Survey perceptions do not prove AI improves clinical outcomes.

    Confidence: High

    Hidden Limitations / Our Critical View¶

    These are our PhD-analysis points.

    Critical Point Why It Matters
    Survey evidence, not clinical validation It shows adoption attitudes, not model effectiveness.
    No India-specific breakdown confirmed It cannot directly describe Indian IVF clinic readiness.
    Journal/source level should be used cautiously Useful for deployment context, but not a primary clinical-outcome study.
    Strong interest does not equal trustworthy implementation Adoption requires training, governance and validation.
    Reported outcome improvement is perception-based It should not be cited as proof of live-birth improvement.

    Confidence: High

    Future Work Suggested¶

    This paper supports future work on:

    • clinician training for AI use in IVF
    • cost-sensitive AI implementation
    • ethical and legal frameworks
    • human oversight to reduce over-reliance
    • evidence generation for real patient outcomes
    • standardized AI adoption definitions
    • region-specific readiness studies

    For our PhD, this supports adding clinician review and usability evaluation if we build an explainable CDSS.

    Confidence: High

    Usefulness For Our PhD¶

    Usefulness: High for deployment and faculty-presentation context

    This paper helps answer faculty questions such as:

    • Are IVF specialists actually using AI?
    • Where is AI mostly used?
    • What prevents AI adoption?
    • Do clinicians trust AI?
    • What risks do fertility specialists see?

    It supports our argument that a useful PhD contribution should include clinician-facing explanation, validation and practical adoption factors.

    What This Paper Does Not Prove¶

    This paper does not prove that:

    • AI improves live-birth rates
    • AI embryo selection is clinically superior
    • AI tools are ready for Indian IVF clinics
    • clinician interest equals clinical trust
    • survey responses represent all fertility specialists
    • AI should be deployed without validation

    Thesis-Ready Citation Sentences¶

    Shoham et al. (2025) compared global surveys of IVF professionals from 2022 and 2025 and found that AI use increased, with 53.22% of 2025 respondents reporting regular or occasional use.

    The study found that embryo selection remained the dominant AI application, while cost, lack of training, over-reliance on technology, errors and privacy were major barriers or risks.

    For this PhD, the paper supports the need for clinician-facing explainability, usability evaluation, ethical safeguards and real-world validation before AI-based IVF decision support can be responsibly adopted.
