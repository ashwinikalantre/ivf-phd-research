# Research Problem

## Simple Problem Statement

Many IVF studies use AI or machine learning to predict pregnancy, live birth, embryo quality, or treatment response.

But many of these studies stop at prediction.

They often do not show:

- whether the model works in another clinic
- why the model made a prediction
- how a doctor should use the prediction
- whether the explanation is understandable
- whether the model fits Indian IVF patients

## Core Research Problem

Current IVF AI models can predict outcomes, but many are not explainable, externally validated, personalized, or clinically usable.

There is a need for an explainable clinical decision support framework that can help doctors and patients understand IVF outcome predictions and use them safely during counseling.

## Why This Is Important

IVF decisions are sensitive.

Patients invest money, time, emotion, and health effort. Doctors also need to make careful decisions about treatment plans, embryo transfer, and counseling.

So an AI model should not only say:

> success probability is 62%

It should also explain:

- which factors increased the probability
- which factors reduced the probability
- which factors are modifiable
- which factors are not modifiable
- how confident the prediction is
- how the doctor can use this information in counseling

## Evidence From Literature

The 35-paper review shows repeated gaps:

| Gap | Frequency |
| --- | ---: |
| External validation gap | 26 |
| Clinical decision support gap | 18 |
| Real-world deployment gap | 9 |
| Trustworthy AI gap | 8 |
| Prospective/RCT validation gap | 7 |
| Single-center study gap | 6 |

## Refined Research Problem

IVF AI research needs a framework that combines:

- outcome prediction
- explainable AI
- personalized decision support
- multimodal clinical and embryological data
- validation-aware evaluation
- clinician review

This is the main reason for the proposed PhD direction.
