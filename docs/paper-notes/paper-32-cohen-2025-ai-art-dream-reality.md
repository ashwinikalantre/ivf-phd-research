## Why This Paper Matters

This paper matters because it is not trying to sell one model. It critically discusses the difference between AI promise and actual clinical evidence in assisted reproductive technology.

For our PhD, this paper is useful as a supervisor-level framing source. It helps us avoid overclaiming and supports a cautious research direction:

- AI in IVF is promising, but not automatically clinically useful
- retrospective performance does not prove real-world benefit
- validation, workflow fit and clinical governance matter
- clinical decision support must be evaluated carefully
- claims should focus on what evidence actually supports

This paper strengthens the logic of our thesis direction: the research should not be only about building another model; it should be about explainable, validated and clinically meaningful decision support.

## Main Findings

The paper is useful for these high-level conclusions:

- AI has potential to improve ART efficiency and standardization.
- Many ART AI applications still lack rigorous clinical validation.
- Clinical utility should be assessed separately from technical performance.
- Real-world adoption requires careful integration into IVF laboratory and clinical workflows.
- AI claims in ART should be realistic and evidence-based.

Confidence: **Medium-High** because these findings are supported by the paper title, accessible source summaries and existing project extraction, but not by full-text extraction in this session.
