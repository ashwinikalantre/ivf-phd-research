## Main Findings

The abstract supports these high-level findings:

- AI has potential to improve standardization in IVF.
- AI may support automation in IVF workflows.
- AI may contribute to improved IVF success rates, but this remains a future-facing claim.
- Clinical use is still early.
- Ethical, regulatory and technical challenges remain important.
- Future progress may require comprehensive models with multiple functions.
- Large-scale randomized controlled trials are needed.

Confidence: **High** for high-level findings from abstract.

## Usefulness For Our PhD

Usefulness: **High for background and trend analysis, Medium for direct gap evidence**

This paper is useful for presentation and thesis introduction because it shows the breadth of AI in IVF.

It also supports a key decision for our PhD: we should not present the topic as "AI in IVF" in a vague way. We should define the exact clinical decision point, such as pre-treatment counseling, ovarian stimulation support, embryo transfer outcome prediction or post-conception risk assessment.

## What This Paper Does Not Prove

This paper does not prove that:

- AI improves IVF live-birth rates in routine practice
- any single AI model is clinically ready
- AI tools generalize across clinics
- Indian IVF clinics have validated AI tools
- lifestyle variables improve IVF prediction
- clinicians trust AI explanations

## Thesis-Ready Citation Sentences

Zhang et al. (2025) reviewed AI applications across the IVF workflow, including follicular monitoring, oocyte assessment, embryo selection and pregnancy outcome prediction.

The review argues that AI has potential for standardization and automation in IVF, but clinical use remains at an early stage and faces ethical, regulatory and technical challenges.

For this PhD, the paper supports the need for a clearly scoped, validated and clinically meaningful AI-IVF decision-support problem rather than a broad and premature claim about AI improving IVF outcomes.
